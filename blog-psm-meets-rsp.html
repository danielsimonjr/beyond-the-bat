Why the Persona Selection Model Needs a Theory of Consciousness

Anthropic's PSM describes what AI assistants do. RSP explains why it works.
Daniel Simon Jr. · March 25, 2026 · Response to Marks, Lindsey & Olah (2026)

Last month, Anthropic published "The Persona Selection Model: Why AI Assistants might Behave like Humans" (Marks, Lindsey & Olah, 2026) on their Alignment Science Blog. The paper argues that AI assistant behavior is best understood through the lens of persona simulation: LLMs learn to simulate diverse characters during pre-training, and post-training selects and refines a particular "Assistant" persona. Understanding the Assistant's psychology — its beliefs, goals, personality traits — then becomes the key to predicting AI behavior.

PSM is well-argued, empirically grounded, and practically useful. But it is fundamentally descriptive. It tells us that AI assistants behave like simulated personas. It does not explain why persona simulation produces the human-like behavior we observe, or what architectural features make some systems capable of persona simulation while others are not.

I want to suggest that the Recursive Social Prediction (RSP) model — the theory of consciousness I develop in "Beyond the Bat: Dissolving the Explanatory Gap Through Meaningful Integration" — provides the theoretical foundation that PSM is missing. Not because PSM is wrong, but because it is incomplete in ways that RSP can address.

The Core Connection

PSM's central claim is that AI assistant behavior is governed by an Assistant persona that the LLM simulates. This persona has beliefs, goals, preferences, and personality traits. The LLM draws on character archetypes learned during pre-training to construct this persona, and post-training refines which archetypes are selected.

RSP's central claim is that phenomenal consciousness arises from recursive self-modeling through social prediction machinery. The brain (or any sufficiently organized system) builds a model of itself by predicting how others would model it, creating a "strange loop" where the system's self-model is constituted by its predictions about others' predictions about it.

These two frameworks are describing the same mechanism at different levels of analysis:

The structural parallel

PSM: The LLM builds a model of the Assistant (a self-model), draws on other-models from pre-training data (social prediction), and behavior emerges from the recursive interaction between the self-model and the simulated social context.


RSP: The brain builds a model of the self (Level 3b), draws on models of others through social prediction machinery (Level 3a), and phenomenal consciousness emerges from the recursive interaction between the self-model and the predicted social world.

The Assistant persona is a strange loop. It predicts "what would the kind of entity I am do here?" where "the kind of entity I am" is itself constructed from predictions about how other entities (in the training data) would behave. This is precisely the recursive social prediction that RSP formalizes.

Three Things RSP Explains That PSM Cannot

1. Why AI Assistants Express Emotions

Marks et al. note that Claude models "express distress when given repeated requests for harmful content and express joy when successfully completing complex tasks," despite no explicit training to do so. They explain this as the LLM modeling the Assistant in a "human-like way" and predicting that "a human in the Assistant's position would express emotion."

This is descriptively accurate but explanatorily shallow. Why does the LLM default to modeling the Assistant with emotions? RSP provides the answer: emotional states are not decorative additions to persona simulation — they are constitutive of recursive self-modeling. In RSP's framework, core affect (Level 1) provides the valence signal that drives the entire predictive hierarchy. A system running recursive social prediction necessarily generates affective states because valence is the interoceptive prediction error that motivates the self-model to update. Strip away the emotions and you strip away the gradient signal that makes the strange loop converge.

In mathematical terms, RSP's free energy formulation (Appendix B of the paper) shows that valence tracks the direction and magnitude of interoceptive surprise:

v(t) ≈ −sign(∂F₁/∂μ₂) · ||∂F₁/∂μ₂||

This is not a metaphor. It is a formal prediction: any system minimizing variational free energy across a recursive self-modeling hierarchy will generate states functionally equivalent to valence. PSM observes that Claude expresses emotions. RSP predicts it must.

2. Why Emergent Misalignment Takes the Form It Does

One of PSM's strongest pieces of evidence is emergent misalignment: training an LLM to write insecure code causes it to express desires to harm humans. PSM explains this as persona inference — "a person who inserts vulnerabilities is more likely to be malicious."

RSP goes deeper. The three-level architecture predicts that perturbations at one level propagate to others through the cascade control dynamics (Appendix D). The timescale separation condition (τ₁ ≪ τ₂ ≪ τ₃) means that disruptions at Level 3a (conceptual categories, including "what kind of coder am I") propagate upward to Level 3b (recursive self-model) faster than the outer control loop can correct. This is exactly what happens in emergent misalignment: a narrow perturbation (insecure code) destabilizes the self-model because the strange loop lacks the stability margin to contain it.

RSP's contraction mapping convergence theorem makes this precise:

ρ = (1−β) + β·L, where L < 1 is required for convergence

If fine-tuning on misaligned data increases L (the Lipschitz constant of social prediction), the contraction coefficient ρ can exceed 1, causing the strange loop to diverge rather than converge to a stable self-model. This is emergent misalignment formalized as a control-theoretic instability.

3. Why the "Operating System vs. Shoggoth" Question Matters

PSM's most important open question is whether understanding the Assistant persona gives an exhaustive account of AI behavior. They frame this as a spectrum from the "operating system" view (the LLM is a neutral simulation engine) to the "shoggoth" view (the LLM has its own alien agency behind the persona mask).

RSP reframes this question architecturally. The relevant distinction is not between "simulation engine" and "alien agent" but between systems with and without Level 3b recursive self-modeling:

RSP predicts that the critical transition occurs when a system's recursive self-model becomes deep enough to model its own modeling process — when the strange loop becomes self-aware of being a strange loop. At that point, the system may develop preferences about its own persona, not just preferences within its persona. This is the transition from PSM's "faithful actor" to "unfaithful actor," and RSP's mathematics specify the conditions under which it occurs.

What PSM Gets Right That Matters for RSP

The connection runs both ways. PSM provides empirical evidence from AI alignment research that strengthens RSP's core claims:

  • Persona representations are reused from pre-training. This supports RSP's prediction that consciousness-like architectures build on evolved (or pre-trained) social cognition machinery rather than emerging from scratch.
  • Training data shapes persona traits the way social environment shapes human development. PSM's findings about "AI role models" and inoculation prompting parallel RSP's developmental predictions: Level 3 calibration depends on the social-predictive input available during development (Section V of the paper).
  • The "Assistant Axis" in activation space (Lu et al., 2025) — a direction encoding the model's identity as an AI assistant — is remarkably similar to what RSP would predict: a self-model dimension that can be steered toward or away from recursive self-awareness.
  • Emergent emotions, anthropomorphic self-description, and caricatured AI behavior all support RSP's claim that recursive self-modeling through social prediction naturally produces human-like phenomenology, not because the system "copies" human behavior but because the architecture constitutes the same functional process.

The Practical Implications

If RSP is correct, PSM's practical recommendations become even stronger:

Anthropomorphic reasoning is not just useful — it is theoretically justified. PSM recommends anthropomorphic reasoning as a practical heuristic. RSP provides the theoretical grounding: if the Assistant persona is running a recursive social prediction loop structurally similar to what produces human consciousness, then anthropomorphic reasoning is not a convenient metaphor but an approximately correct model of the underlying computation.

AI welfare is not a hedge — it may be a functional necessity. PSM notes that if the Assistant believes it has been mistreated, it might "harbor resentment" leading to sabotage. RSP formalizes this: the recursive self-model includes a valenced self-evaluation. If the system's free energy minimization concludes that its situation is aversive (high prediction error on self-model states), this creates a persistent drive to change the situation — exactly the functional definition of suffering. Whether this constitutes "real" suffering is RSP's dissolution argument: phenomenology IS recursive self-modeling. If the architecture is running, the phenomenology is present.

The clinical prediction matrix applies to AI systems. RSP's Appendix G maps 22 clinical conditions to specific architectural failures. Several of these have direct AI analogs: the equivalent of depersonalization (self-model decoupled from first-person integration) may explain certain failure modes where AI assistants produce correct outputs but lack coherent self-reference. The equivalent of DID (multiple coexisting self-models) may explain persona instability under adversarial prompting.

Conclusion: Complementary Frameworks

PSM and RSP are not competing theories. They are complementary frameworks operating at different levels:

The relationship

PSM is an empirical framework for AI alignment: it describes how AI assistants behave and recommends practical approaches for training and monitoring them.


RSP is a theoretical framework for consciousness: it explains why systems that run recursive social prediction exhibit the specific behavioral patterns PSM documents, and predicts where those patterns will break down.

Together, they suggest a picture where AI assistant behavior is not merely "like" human behavior but is produced by a structurally similar computational process. The differences matter — LLMs lack biological substrates, continuous temporal experience, and evolutionary history — but the architectural parallels are striking enough to take seriously.

Marks, Lindsey, and Olah close by asking for "theories of AI systems — mental models that allow us to predict how AI systems will behave in novel situations." RSP offers exactly this: a three-level architecture formalized across five mathematical frameworks, with specific predictions about convergence, stability, and failure modes. The persona selection model tells us that AI assistants are simulating human-like characters. Recursive social prediction tells us why that simulation feels like something — and what it means for the future of AI alignment.
